ARCS Component Attention (A)

Attention: the entry point of motivation

In Keller’s ARCS Model, attention is not simply “making learning fun.” It refers to designing conditions that help learners notice the learning task, become mentally engaged, and remain engaged long enough to learn.

In e-learning, attention is especially fragile because learners often study alone, face constant distractions, and can leave the lesson at any moment. That is why attention functions as a motivational gate: if attention is not gained, relevance, confidence, and satisfaction cannot effectively operate.

Sub-categories of Attention

Keller describes attention as something that can be intentionally triggered and sustained through different mechanisms. In instructional design, attention can be supported through:

A1
Perceptual Arousal

Gaining attention through novelty, surprise, or visual changes that interrupt routine thinking.

  • Unexpected examples or visuals
  • Short, strong hooks
  • Pattern breaks in slides/videos
A2
Inquiry Arousal

Creating curiosity by presenting a question, problem, or gap that learners want to resolve.

  • Prediction questions
  • Small puzzles or scenarios
  • “What would you do?” prompts
A3
Variability

Sustaining attention by changing the pace, format, or interaction type so learners do not disengage.

  • Mix short videos + questions
  • Visual + text alternation
  • Micro-practice moments

Attention Design Strategies in E-learning

Below is a practical checklist that translates the attention component into concrete design actions. These strategies help learners begin and continue the learning task.

1) Hook with purpose

Open with a hook that is directly related to the learning objective (not random entertainment).

Example: “Which ARCS component fails most often in online learning—and why?”
2) Ask inquiry questions

Use prediction questions to create curiosity and a reason to keep watching or reading.

Example: “Before we define Attention, predict one reason learners drop out of online courses.”
3) Plan micro-interactions

Add short actions every few minutes so learners are not passive: click, choose, reflect, or type.

Example: “Choose the best hook for this lesson” (A/B/C).
4) Reduce distraction with signaling

Keep screens focused and use visual cues to highlight what matters most.

Example: One key idea per screen + bolded keywords + short sections.
