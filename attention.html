Attention (A): the entry point of motivation

In e-learning, learners can leave at any moment. Attention is the first “gate”: if we don’t capture and sustain attention, the rest of ARCS (Relevance, Confidence, Satisfaction) has no chance to work.

Attention ≠ entertainment

Attention design means learners (1) notice the key idea, (2) stay engaged long enough to process and practice, and (3) focus on what matters.

Sub-categories of Attention (Keller)

Keller describes attention as something designers can plan. In practice, attention strategies often fall into: Perceptual Arousal, Inquiry Arousal, and Variability.

Why visuals matter
Perceptual + Inquiry Arousal often start with a cue

In online lessons, learners often scan quickly. A clear visual cue can function as a “pattern break” and a prompt for curiosity—then your design must guide attention toward the learning goal.

A1
Perceptual Arousal

Capture attention through novelty or surprise (visual, audio, or pattern break).

  • Unexpected statistic
  • Short “pattern-break” animation
  • Strong contrast to highlight key content
A2
Inquiry Arousal

Trigger curiosity by creating a “knowledge gap” learners want to close.

  • Prediction question
  • Mini case: “What would you do?”
  • Reveal-after-choice feedback
A3
Variability

Sustain attention by varying format and activity (without losing focus).

  • Micro-video → quick check
  • Diagram → short task
  • Interactive element every few minutes
Design rule of thumb

In e-learning, aim for a small interaction or decision point every 2–4 minutes (click, choose, predict, drag, reflect). That keeps learners cognitively active.

Attention Toolkit (practical strategies)

Use these strategies when designing screens, modules, or lessons. Each item includes a quick “how to implement” tip for online learning.

1) Define the attention goal
⌄

Decide what learners must notice right now (a concept, misconception, or skill). This prevents “interesting but irrelevant” elements.

Implementation: Add a one-sentence “This screen is about…” at the top of your storyboard.
2) Create an opening hook
⌄

Start with a short scenario, surprising fact, or relatable problem connected to the lesson outcome.

Implementation: Use a 10–15 second “problem prompt” before any definitions.
3) Trigger inquiry with questions
⌄

Use guiding questions or prediction prompts to create curiosity: “What do you think will happen?”

Implementation: Add a “predict → reveal” interaction before showing the correct model/example.
4) Add planned variability
⌄

Change format periodically—micro-video, diagram, short reading, or mini task—so attention doesn’t fade.

Implementation: “Explain → show → do” pattern: 60–90 seconds each.
5) Build interaction moments
⌄

Insert simple actions: click-to-reveal, choose an option, drag-and-drop, or short reflection. Interaction turns learners from viewers into participants.

Implementation: Use “one-click checks” (single question) between segments.
6) Reduce distractions with signaling
⌄

Highlight key ideas using headings, spacing, and visual cues. Remove unnecessary text/visuals so attention stays on what matters.

Implementation: One key message per screen. Use bold/contrast for the “must notice” line.

E-learning Examples (how Attention looks on screen)

Even without special images, you can design attention through structure and interaction. Below are concrete examples you can directly apply to an online lesson.

Example 1: “Hook → Prompt”

Start with a surprising statement and a prediction question.

Hook: “Most online learners stop after the first 2 minutes.”
Prompt: “What do you think causes the drop—content, distraction, or low relevance?”
Example 2: “Micro-task”

After a short explanation, add a quick action to keep cognition active.

Task: “Click the best definition of ‘Inquiry Arousal’.”
Feedback: Immediate explanation strengthens confidence too.
Example 3: “Signaling”

Use structure and minimalism so learners know what matters.

Rule: 1 key message per screen.
Signal: Bold the “must-know” line; remove decorative clutter.
