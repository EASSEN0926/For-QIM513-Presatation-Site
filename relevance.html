Relevance (R): “Why should I care?”

Even if learners are attentive, motivation drops when they do not see value. Relevance connects learning to goals, identity, experience, and future use—especially important in e-learning where learners study independently.

Sub-categories & Strategies E-learning Examples Relevance Builder
Visual cue
Relevance connects learning to goals and real life

Figure: Learners persist when they can see how learning supports goals and future use.

Key idea
Relevance is engineered

Relevance is not “obvious” to learners. Designers must explain purpose, connect to experience, and show how learning can be used.

Clarify purpose
What will learners be able to do—and why it matters.
Connect to context
Use examples that match learners’ roles or experiences.
Show future usefulness
Demonstrate real applications in school or workplace.
Why it matters online
In e-learning, learners often ask “Is this worth my time?” Relevance answers that question repeatedly.

Sub-categories of Relevance (Keller)

Relevance strategies help learners connect new content to personal goals and meaningful outcomes. In practice, relevance often becomes stronger when designers guide learners through: goals → personal connection → future use.

Quick framing
Make relevance visible, not assumed

A simple “why this matters” callout can dramatically improve persistence in online learning. Use goal language, familiar examples, and a future-use scenario.

R
R1
Goal Orientation

Explain the learning goal and how success will be demonstrated.

  • “By the end, you can…” statements
  • Rubric highlights
  • Example of a strong output
R2
Motive Matching

Align activities with what learners care about (interests, needs, values).

  • Choice of examples
  • Role-based paths
  • Optional challenge tasks
R3
Familiarity

Connect new content to prior knowledge or common experiences.

  • Analogies and examples
  • “You may have seen…” cues
  • Bridge from known → new
R4
Future Usefulness

Show how learning can be used beyond the lesson (transfer).

  • Workplace scenarios
  • Academic applications
  • “Next time you…” prompts
Design rule of thumb

Don’t just state relevance once. Reinforce it repeatedly: at the start (purpose), during learning (connections), and near the end (application).

Pro tip

Strong relevance messages typically include: learner identity + purpose + use case. Example: “As a new analyst, you’ll use this to…” or “In your next assignment, this helps you…”

E-learning Examples (university + corporate)

These examples show how relevance can be built into an online lesson—not just stated once.

Example 1: University

A research methods module starts with a clear academic purpose.

Why it matters: “This skill will help you design your final project study and interpret results correctly.”
Relevance strategy: Goal Orientation + Future Usefulness
Example 2: Corporate

A cybersecurity lesson uses role-based framing.

Role frame: “As a team member handling client files, you’ll apply this to avoid phishing and data leaks.”
Relevance strategy: Motive Matching + Future Usefulness
Example 3: Familiarity bridge

Start from what learners already know to reduce distance.

Bridge: “You’ve probably used online quizzes—now we’ll look at why feedback timing affects motivation.”
Relevance strategy: Familiarity
Do / Don’t (Relevance)
Do
  • State purpose in learner language (“You will be able to…”)
  • Use examples tied to roles and real tasks
  • Show future use (assignment, job task, real scenario)
Don’t
  • Assume relevance is obvious
  • Use generic examples with no context
  • Explain relevance once and never return to it
