C (Confidence)

Required section

“I can succeed.”

Confidence is about learners’ belief that success is achievable. In e-learning, confidence is tightly connected to clear expectations, structured progression, support, and feedback. When learners feel lost or overwhelmed, motivation collapses—even if the content is interesting and relevant.

Visual cue

Figure: Confidence increases when expectations are clear and feedback explains how to improve.

Key idea
Confidence is designed

Learners build confidence when they know what “success” looks like, get early wins, and receive support that makes difficulty feel manageable.

Clarity
Clear criteria, examples, and evaluation rules.
Progression
Simple → complex tasks with visible progress.
Support
Scaffolds + feedback + opportunities to retry.
Common risk online
If learners don’t know what to do next, they pause—then they leave. Confidence design prevents that.

✓Does feedback explain why and how to improve?

Sub-categories of Confidence (Keller)

Required section

Confidence strategies help learners predict that effort can lead to success. In practice, designers build confidence through: learning requirements, success opportunities, and personal control.

Quick framing
Make success predictable

Confidence grows when learners can answer three questions during e-learning: What’s expected? Can I practice safely? How do I improve?

C
Design move
Show requirements

Post a rubric/checklist early, plus one high-quality example.

Design move
Create early wins

Start with simpler tasks, then increase difficulty gradually.

Design move
Make help normal

Add hints, retries, and feedback explanations—reduce fear of failure.

C1
Learning Requirements

Learners feel more confident when they understand expectations and evaluation.

  • Clear success criteria
  • Examples of strong responses
  • Rubrics or checklists
C2
Success Opportunities

Structure tasks so learners experience early wins and gradual difficulty.

  • Progressive difficulty
  • Guided practice before independent work
  • Multiple attempts / retries
C3
Personal Control

Give learners choices that increase autonomy and reduce helplessness.

  • Control pacing (pause/rewind)
  • Optional support resources
  • Choose pathway or difficulty level
Design rule of thumb

Confidence grows when difficulty feels predictable and survivable. If learners don’t know what to do next—or why they failed—motivation drops quickly online.

E-learning Examples (how Confidence is built)

Below are practical examples showing how confidence support looks in online learning screens and activities.

Example 1: Clear criteria

Before a quiz or assignment, show “what counts as correct.”

Criteria: “You are correct if you identify (1) the ARCS component and (2) a matching strategy.”
Confidence link: Learning Requirements
Example 2: Scaffolding

Provide guided practice before asking learners to perform independently.

Sequence: Worked example → partially guided task → independent task.
Confidence link: Success Opportunities
Example 3: Learner control

Allow learners to choose pace and access optional help resources.

Control: “Replay video”, “Try again”, “View hint”, “Skip to review”.
Confidence link: Personal Control

Do / Don’t (Confidence)
Do
  • Show criteria + examples early
  • Start simple, then increase difficulty
  • Give feedback that explains “why”
  • Offer retries and optional hints
Don’t
  • Hide evaluation rules
  • Jump into hard tasks immediately
  • Only show “right/wrong” without explanation
  • Remove learner control in e-learning
